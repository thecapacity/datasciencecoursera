Summary

Using a linear regression model, based on the mtcars dataset provided by R, our model found that a car with manual transmission is more effieicnt per gallon than a car with automatic transmission ( 24.39 vs. 17.15). This estimate, that am represents two distinct populations, was validated with a p.value of 0.0014. It was also discovered that the weight (wt) also plays an importat role as well. We only superficially explored interactions across variables. Based on an assumption power indicators may be correlated with wt weight, such as cyl or engine shape vs, the potential exists for further exploration, including whether hp or dispis a better predictor than cyl.

Analysis

I am interested in exploring the relationship between a set of variables and and an efficiency outcome, in miles per gallon (MPG), in the cars data collected. We are particularly interested in the following two questions:

  1. Is an automatic or manual transmission better for MPG? - Answer: Manual

  2. Quantify the MPG difference between automatic and manual transmissions. - Answer: 7.24

Approach

Note: Pre-processing is minimal, and is included in the markdown file - but not shown - to reduce overall page count.

Based on my expertise, I believe cylinders cyl would be another key factor to consider in addition to am type.

Model 1: However, we begin with the most parsimonious model then invetgiate other variables:

fit1 <- lm(mpg ~ am, mtcars) #`am1` represnets a manual transmission
summary(fit1) #output hidden for pagecount

The model shows that am is significant at 0.001 level, with a t value of 4.106, in influencing mpg. However, the Adjusted R-squared value is only 0.3385, which means that the model can only predict about 33.85% of the mpg variable. Clearly some improvement is desired. The residual deiviance (720.9) seems high, but we will save more thorough review once we feel confidant in the model.

Model 2: We then felt it would be useful to compare predicting mpg as a function of all variables:

fit2 <- lm(mpg ~ . - 1, mtcars) #origin not included to make factor comparisons easier
summary(fit2) #output hidden for pagecount

Here we see that the estimated coefficient for wt of -4.53, with a signficance code of 0.1, hp also has similar significance, but only a -0.07 coefficient. Also interesting, is that impact by number of cylinders seems to be counter to an expected result, i.e. cyl4 and cyl8 show similar coefficients, though all cyl values have large margins for Std. Error. This impact seems counter to our intuition, and we believe this is an important indicater for future exploration. In addition the R-squared value of 0.991 too high to be reasonable.

Model 3: Next, we used a model using am, wt, and cyl. This was selected due to the factors mentioned above, excluding hp because of a 0.659 corrlation with wt, and the small coefficient mentioned earlier.

fit3 <- lm(mpg ~ am + wt + cyl, mtcars) 
summary(fit3) #output hidden for pagecount

From this model, we saw that wt and cyl were significant at 0.01 levels. We also saw a more intuitive relationship between mpg and cyl, specifically that mpg decreases more for cyl8 than cyl4. Interestingly, am was not deemed significant; we assume this may be the result of an interaction between am likely with wt.

Therefore our final model contains these factors and the interaction (wt:am):

fit4_final <- lm(mpg ~ am + cyl + wt + am:wt, mtcars)
summary(fit4_final) #$coef #abbreviated output for pagecount

All factors are significant and align with relationships automotive enthuiasts would expect; that MPG goes up for manual cars (am == 1) and down as cyl increases. With a residual variance of 2.3037739 and an Adjusted R-squared of 0.8538884 this model seems to be quite accurate and balances simplicity. A confidence interval confint(fit4_final) suggests some challenges; because the cyl6 interval includes 0. One followup would be whether cyl interacts with other variables like wt.

The appendix plots support the expected residual behavior. There is no consistent Residuals vs Fitted pattern, and the Normal Q-Q plot shows points close along the line, suggesting normal distribution.
